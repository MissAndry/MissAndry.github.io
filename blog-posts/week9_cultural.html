Actionable Asks

We're told that all questions are good questions, but I've got some unfortunate news for you: not all questions are created equal. While it's important to ask questions when you are uncertain of something and no one should be afraid to ask for help, there are ways to formulate questions to garner an informative and helpful answer.

First, let's look at examples of bad questions:

  • Why isn't this working?
  • How do I fix this?
  • Is it possible make this?

These questions, often the initial thoughts of the frustrated, won't get the asker very far. Either they won't receive any responses, or they will be so inundated with information that anything helpful gets lost. The basic problem with all of these questions is they're too general! There's no context for "this" or "working" or "fix." On the first question, a better phrasing might be:

  • Why isn't this factorial function returning the proper factorial of one?

That question is much better because it defines what the asker means by "this" - a factorial function - and what would constitute its "working" - giving the proper factorial of one. Hopefully the asker would also include a code snippet so another person could see what needs debugging.

While the edited question is certainly better than the first one, it could still use some work.

A good question should leave no mystery about what it refers to and how much the asker already knows about the topic.
